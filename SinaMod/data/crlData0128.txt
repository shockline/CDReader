    What do Wu-Maddison numbers show? First, unlike official numbers and economists’ estimates, which tend to argue about minutia of decimal points (should the growth be 6.3% or 6.5%?), Wu-Maddison estimates show a much higher degree of volatility than is evident in official numbers. Whenever the business cycles in China and the US diverge,as it is happening now, the HKD peg will come under pressure and questions about its longevity willarise. However, the peg is likely to stay on for several reasons. The Hong Kong government hasample FX reserves to defend the peg and the currency board has withstood more intense pressures inthe past via large adjustments in domestic markets (interest rate, equity, real estate, and labor) andcountercyclical fiscal policy. These remain in place. But more importantly, the HKD peg remainsone of the most visible symbols of the “one-country, two-systems” relationship between theMainland and Hong Kong. The benefits of safeguarding the credibility of this relationship are likelyto continue to outweigh the costs of adjusting via painful domestic price changes as in the past. Allthat said, pressures on the peg can push HKD forward rates significantly away from the spot FXtrading band (i.e., the so-called convertibility undertaking) for a sustained period e.g., over 2006-07.
